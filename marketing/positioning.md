## The customer test
A literate adult chooses Kvasir over Character.AI because Kvasir has a creator economy: someone built that Hamlet chatbot, designed that contest, and earns when players engage. Character.AI is pure consumption — nothing is created, nobody earns, and the characters aren't grounded in the actual text.

## The honest gap
Nobody has heard of Kvasir. The product exists, the mechanics work — but there is no distribution engine yet. The first customer doesn't say "this isn't for me." They say "what is this?"

## The core bet
The next 6 months are about creating one shareable contest format that spreads without paid acquisition — the Wordle moment for literary AI. Not more features. One loop that players finish, screenshot, and send to a friend.

## Market scope
Literary AI games. Word games and chatbots grounded in classic texts. This excludes generic AI chat, generic edtech, and generic writing tools. It wins a specific shelf in a specific reader's mind.

## Positioning statement

> We help literary creators publish AI word games and contests that players return to daily — unlike Character.AI, where creators don't exist and nothing is won.

## Founder's first draft
> "We help people (readers and players) to fill their spare time, we help creators to get monetization from it"

**What changed:** The draft led with passive time-filling ("spare time") and vague creator reward ("monetization"). The final flips the frame: creators are the engine, players are the habit, and the alternative is named. "Spare time" positions Kvasir as optional. "Return to daily" positions it as a ritual — which is the actual bet.
