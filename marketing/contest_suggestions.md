# Suggested Contests: Public Domain Books

Books selected for: strong characters, debatable themes, cultural recognition, broad appeal, and search volume.

## Tier 1: Highest Engagement Potential (Launch First)

### 1. Pride and Prejudice -- Jane Austen (1813)
- Universally beloved. Huge BookTok presence
- Rich debates: "Is Mr. Darcy actually toxic?" drives massive engagement
- Massive search volume for quizzes and discussion questions
- **Contest angle:** "Create the most insightful conversation with Elizabeth Bennet"

### 2. The Great Gatsby -- F. Scott Fitzgerald (1925, public domain since 2021)
- Required reading in many schools -- built-in audience
- "Is Gatsby a romantic or a stalker?" debate
- American Dream themes resonate across demographics
- **Contest angle:** "Debate the American Dream with AI Gatsby"

### 3. Frankenstein -- Mary Shelley (1818)
- Perfect for AI-themed discussion ("Who is the real monster?")
- Ties into current AI ethics conversations
- Appeals to sci-fi/horror fans beyond typical literary audiences
- **Contest angle:** "Explore AI ethics through Frankenstein's lens"

### 4. Dracula -- Bram Stoker (1897)
- Epistolary format creates natural discussion points
- Horror/gothic fans are highly engaged online
- Ties into massive vampire fiction fandom
- **Contest angle:** "Interview Count Dracula about immortality"

### 5. Sherlock Holmes -- Arthur Conan Doyle (1892)
- Mystery/detective fans are a huge, engaged community
- AI can play Watson, or challenge users to solve cases
- Gamification fits naturally -- multiple stories = multiple contests
- **Contest angle:** "Solve the case with AI Watson"

## Tier 2: Strong Discussion Generators

### 6. Crime and Punishment -- Dostoevsky (1866)
- Moral philosophy goldmine. "Was Raskolnikov justified?"
- Appeals to psychology enthusiasts
- **Contest angle:** "Put Raskolnikov on trial"

### 7. The Picture of Dorian Gray -- Oscar Wilde (1890)
- Vanity/morality themes resonate with social media era
- Wilde's wit makes entertaining AI conversation
- LGBTQ+ literary significance draws engaged audience
- **Contest angle:** "Debate vanity and morality with Dorian"

### 8. Jane Eyre -- Charlotte Bronte (1847)
- Strong feminist themes for modern discussion
- "Is Rochester a good love interest?" debate
- Pairs with Pride and Prejudice for "19th-century romance" series
- **Contest angle:** "Discuss independence and love with Jane"

### 9. The Count of Monte Cristo -- Alexandre Dumas (1844)
- One of the greatest revenge stories ever told
- "Was Edmond's revenge justified?" drives heated discussion
- Adventure fans love it
- **Contest angle:** "Plan your revenge with the Count"

### 10. Alice in Wonderland -- Lewis Carroll (1865)
Already on the platform as "A Mad Tea Party." Expand with:
- Full book contest, not just the tea party chapter
- Philosophy and logic puzzle angle
- **Contest angle:** "Navigate Wonderland's logic puzzles"

## Tier 3: Niche but Passionate Audiences

### 11. Meditations -- Marcus Aurelius (~180 AD)
- Stoicism is massively trending (r/Stoicism: 800K+ members)
- Self-improvement crowd. Short passages = daily discussion format
- **Contest angle:** "Apply Stoic wisdom to modern problems"

### 12. The Art of War -- Sun Tzu (~5th century BC)
- Business/strategy community loves it
- Appeals to non-traditional book audiences
- **Contest angle:** "Strategic thinking challenge with Sun Tzu"

### 13. Metamorphosis -- Franz Kafka (1915)
- Short enough to read in one sitting
- Existential themes resonate with younger readers
- **Contest angle:** "Explore existential dread with Gregor Samsa"

### 14. A Christmas Carol -- Charles Dickens (1843)
- Seasonal marketing opportunity (November-December traffic spike)
- Universally known story
- **Contest angle:** "Convince Scrooge to change" (seasonal event)

### 15. The Odyssey -- Homer (~8th century BC)
- Foundation of Western literature
- Each book/episode = natural contest unit
- Percy Jackson fans discovering the source material
- **Contest angle:** "Navigate the Odyssey one adventure at a time"

### 16. Moby-Dick -- Herman Melville (1851)
- A classic most people haven't actually read
- "Is Ahab insane or heroic?" drives discussion
- **Contest angle:** "Debate obsession and purpose with Captain Ahab"

### 17. The Republic -- Plato (~375 BC)
- The Allegory of the Cave alone generates endless discussion
- Active online philosophy community
- **Contest angle:** "Design the ideal society with Plato"

### 18. War and Peace -- Leo Tolstoy (1869)
- The "Mount Everest" of reading -- people are motivated to tackle it with AI support
- Chapter-by-chapter contests make it approachable
- Completing it = shareable achievement
- **Contest angle:** "Conquer War and Peace, one chapter at a time"

## Recommended Launch Sequence

| Week | Books | Rationale |
|------|-------|-----------|
| 1-2 | Pride and Prejudice, Sherlock Holmes | Broad appeal, recognizable, fun |
| 3-4 | Frankenstein, The Great Gatsby | AI themes, American culture |
| Month 2 | Dracula, Dorian Gray, Meditations | Niche enthusiast audiences |
| Month 3 | Crime and Punishment, Monte Cristo | Deeper literary engagement |
| Seasonal | A Christmas Carol (December) | Traffic spike opportunity |
| Ongoing | Follow BookTok trends | Ride viral waves |
